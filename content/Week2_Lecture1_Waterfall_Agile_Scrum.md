## 1. The Core Scenario (why methodology matters)

A software company must develop a new application. The team must: **collect requirements → design the system → develop features → test the application → deliver the product.** The whole lecture answers one question: **how should the team organize this work?**

## 3. The Waterfall Model

**Defining traits:**

| Trait | Detail |
|---|---|
| Phase separation | Each phase is **separated** from the others |
| Staffing | Staff are **dedicated to each phase** |
| Document-driven | **Guided by the production of documents** |
| Progress measurement | Progress is measurable based on the **amount of documentation produced** |
| Personnel continuity | Documents **support personnel changes** (a new person can pick up the docs) |

### Waterfall — Negative Aspects (high-value exam list)

| Weakness | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Over-documentation | Too much focus on producing **documents**, less on the actual software product |
| Late delivery | Software is released **only at the end** |
| Limited customer contact | Customer involved **only during the initial phase** (requirements definition) |
| Rigidity | **Changes to requirements are not possible** after the requirements phase ends |

> **The big issue → "Requirements up-front!"** Waterfall demands you fully define requirements before building, which rarely survives contact with reality.

## 4. What Is Agile?

**Founding ideas:**
- There is **no single recipe** that produces perfect software every time. Agile teams have ideas and ground rules that help them make good choices, avoid problems, and deal with problems when they emerge.
- A good developer almost always has **opinions about the whole direction** of the project.
- **Changes are unavoidable.**
- Software is a **high-added-value artefact**; quality is **strongly dependent on people**.

**Agile as a set of methods/methodologies promises:**

| More… | Promises |
|---|---|
| More effective work | On time |
| More efficient | High quality |
| Better decisions | Highly maintainable |
| | User happy |
| | Working normal hours |

### The Agile Model — structural ideas

| Idea | |
|---|---|
| Decomposition | Complex work divided into **small parts** |
| Team structure | Big companies divided into **small teams** |
| Time-boxing | Long projects divided into **lists of tasks** done in a short time |
| Change | **Requirements can change at any time** |

### Agile Software Development — definition

Software is developed in **increments**, with the customer specifying the requirements for each increment. The **highest priority is to satisfy the customer through early and continuous delivery of valuable software.** Agile is **iterative**: one iteration is followed by improvements in the next.

## 5. The Agile Manifesto — The Four Values

> Origin: a group of highly skilled, innovative people started Agile as a **revolution against the "waterfall mindset."** Manifesto: agilemanifesto.org

**The Four Values (memorize the exact "A over B" wording):**

| # | Value (we value… **over**…) |
|---|---|
| 1 | **Individuals and interactions** over processes and tools |
| 2 | **Working software** over comprehensive documentation |
| 3 | **Customer collaboration** over contract negotiation |
| 4 | **Responding to change** over following a plan |

> Trap: the values do **not** say the right-hand items are worthless — they say the left items are valued **more**.

## 6. The Twelve Principles of Agile Development

| # | Principle |
|---|---|
| 1 | Customer satisfaction through **early and continuous** software delivery |
| 2 | **Accommodate changing requirements** throughout development |
| 3 | **Frequent delivery** of working software |
| 4 | **Collaboration** between business stakeholders and developers throughout |
| 5 | **Support, trust, and motivate** the people involved |
| 6 | Enable **face-to-face** interactions |
| 7 | **Working software** is the primary measure of progress |
| 8 | Agile processes support a **consistent (sustainable) development pace** |
| 9 | Attention to **technical detail and design** enhances agility |
| 10 | **Simplicity** — develop just enough to get the job done for right now |
| 11 | **Self-organizing teams** encourage great architectures, requirements, designs |
| 12 | **Regular reflections** on how to become more effective |

## 7. Waterfall vs Agile — The Core Comparison

| Dimension | **Waterfall** | **Agile** |
|---|---|---|
| Structure | Sequential, phase-by-phase | Iterative / incremental |
| Requirements | Fixed up-front | Can change at any time |
| Delivery | Once, at the end | Early & continuous, in increments |
| Customer involvement | Only at the start (requirements) | Throughout the project |
| Primary progress measure | Documentation produced | Working software |
| Handling change | Change resisted/impossible after requirements | Change embraced |
| Documentation | Heavy | "Just enough" |

> **PM insight (slide 21):** For a "waterfall" team, one of the **most complex issues in project management is the transition toward an Agile mindset** — it's a cultural shift, not just a process swap.

## 9. Scrum Methodology

**Scrum is an Agile methodology built on FIVE values:**

| Value | Meaning (exam-ready) |
|---|---|
| **Courage** | Team members have the courage to **stand up for the project** and live by values/principles that benefit it. It takes courage to **ward off constant pushback** from a company whose values clash with Scrum/Agile values. |
| **Commitment** | The team is **committed to the project's goals**; it has the **authority to make decisions** to meet those goals, and everyone can influence how the project is planned and executed. |
| **Respect** | Team members **respect each other** and **trust each other** to do a good job with the work they've taken on. The Scrum Master should find ways to **increase mutual respect**. |
| **Focus** | The team is **focused on the work**; during a sprint it should **not be distracted** by other activities. Switching people among activities **wastes time and money**; not attending a formative activity does not affect a team member's career. |
| **Openness** | Everyone on the team should always be **aware of what you're working on** and how it moves the project toward its current goals. Many companies have a culture that **discourages transparency** — rigid hierarchy that depends on **opaqueness**. |

> Mnemonic for the 5 Scrum values: **C-C-R-F-O** (Courage, Commitment, Respect, Focus, Openness).
